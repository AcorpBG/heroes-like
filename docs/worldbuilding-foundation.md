# Worldbuilding Foundation

Status: design source, not implementation proof.
Date: 2026-04-25.
Slice: world-story-worldbuilding-foundation-10184.

## Purpose

This document establishes the first original world foundation for the game. It gives faction, economy, magic, overworld-object, artifact, visual, and scenario work a shared setting to build from before more JSON implementation, maps, or screen polish.

Heroes 2, Heroes 3, and Oden Era may inform expectations for readable scale, strategic density, and map clarity only. They are not source material for names, lore, factions, maps, art, music, unit identities, or text.

## World Premise

The game is set in **Aurelion Reach**, a broken continental basin where old sky mirrors once regulated seasons, tides, memory, and ore growth. Those mirrors did not fall as one disaster. They cracked over generations, leaving the land with unstable weather, exposed deep metals, prophetic fog banks, sun-scarred crystal fields, haunted waterways, and forests that migrate when old root laws are broken.

The current age is the **Charter War**. Six powers claim they can restore order, but each wants a different future:

- A civic river league wants enforceable roads, tolls, granaries, and beacon law.
- A marsh covenant wants the lowlands free from courthouse maps and mine contracts.
- A solar compact wants to rebuild the mirror network under disciplined calibration.
- A living orchard concord wants the land itself to have veto power over settlement.
- A brass combine wants resource extraction bound by contract, debt, and furnace capacity.
- A fog armada wants mobility, salvage rights, memory trade, and no fixed empire at all.

The playable conflict is not good versus evil. It is a resource, sovereignty, and survival struggle in a world where geography has become political. Every faction is partly right about the crisis and dangerous in how it solves the crisis.

## Tone

The tone is **bright-readable strategy fantasy with hard-edged logistics underneath**.

Player-facing screens should feel adventurous, scenic, and materially grounded: river locks, ferry chains, crystal relays, thorn gates, pressure rails, fog harbors, mine heads, shrines, ruins, and road decisions. The world should not read as grimdark, parody, generic high fantasy, or clean heroic court romance. It should feel like a place where armies march because grain, bridges, mines, contracts, and weather windows matter.

Tone rules:

- Wonder comes from places and systems, not from vague prophecy.
- Magic has visible infrastructure: lenses, bells, writs, root knots, drums, furnaces, mirrors, cairns, and marked roads.
- War leaves practical traces: abandoned tollhouses, burned relay poles, half-flooded forges, ruined nurseries, broken rail beds, salvage camps, ration cairns.
- Humor can exist in incidental object details and hero voice, but the strategic layer should stay sincere.
- Factions should have human-level motives even when their units include beasts, constructs, spirits, or living terrain.

## Geography And Regions

Aurelion Reach is organized around a central basin, broken highlands, and outer seas. Scenario regions should use these as repeatable world grammar instead of arbitrary fantasy biomes.

### 1. The Emberflow Basin

Wide rivers, mill towns, floodplain roads, lock gates, levees, toll bridges, granary islands, and beacon towers. This is the natural home of Embercourt power, but it should include contested mills, sun relay ruins, hidden ferry roads, and old orchard enclaves.

Strategic implications:

- Roads and bridges matter more than raw distance.
- Towns sit at river forks, locks, and granary chains.
- Fights often happen over crossings, mills, signal posts, and supply yards.
- Visual anchors: red beacons, stone weirs, barges, warm civic fire, water wheels, soot on pale masonry.

### 2. The Drowned Marches

Fen, reed seas, drowned roads, peat cuts, blackwater ferries, drum islands, and shrine posts made from old bridge woods. Mireclaw does not simply live here; it controls the rules of movement here.

Strategic implications:

- Dense side paths, ambush pockets, and narrow safe lanes.
- Map objects should often imply risk: ferry tolls, reed caches, beast dens, old causeways, peat pits.
- Travel should feel tempting but unsafe, with rewards visible just off the clean route.
- Visual anchors: low silhouettes, wet hides, chain ferries, green-black water, reed smoke, mudglass glints.

### 3. The Glass Uplands

Sun-struck ridges, lens fields, crystal orchards, prism roads, and buried observatories. Sunvault settlements use angle, resonance, and height to project control across long lanes.

Strategic implications:

- Sightline sites and watch control should be common.
- Strong map positions should look deliberate: terraces, relay towers, lens crowns, calibrated road stones.
- Rewards should skew toward spell access, crystal resources, scouting, and high-quality guarded sites.
- Visual anchors: pale stone, gold-white light, blue-violet crystal, long shadows, mirrored terraces.

### 4. The Walking Green

A migrating forest belt of graft orchards, thorn tolls, pilgrim clearings, root gates, old nurseries, and forbidden logging roads. Thornwake power is not just nature magic; it is law carried by living landscape.

Strategic implications:

- Roads can look partially reclaimed, redirected, or taxed by roots.
- Sites should support recovery, denial, regrowth, and long campaigns.
- Decorations and blockers should feel alive without becoming unreadable.
- Visual anchors: graft tags, root arches, thorn braids, fruit lanterns, pale bark, dark leaves, woven pilgrimage markers.

### 5. The Brass Deeps

Quarries, furnace valleys, pressure rails, debt foundries, brass gantries, worker chapels, and underway mouths. Brasshollow power rises where ore, law, and heat can be made predictable.

Strategic implications:

- Mines and extraction sites are not passive decoration. They should define expansion routes.
- Object clusters should include staged work: survey stakes, pump houses, slag roads, rail spurs, contract halls.
- Travel should have fewer soft rewards and more valuable, guarded industrial points.
- Visual anchors: brass, black iron, hot ceramic, pressure gauges, mine cables, ash-gray roads, warning banners.

### 6. The Veil Coast

Fog harbors, mirror shoals, bell buoys, wreck fields, black-sail slips, obituary vaults, and half-real routes that appear when tide and memory align. Veilmourn controls movement others cannot chart.

Strategic implications:

- Scouting, bypasses, salvage, and ambush routes should be region-defining.
- Water and coast content should be more than borders. It should create alternate route economies.
- Map objects can hold partial information: ghost lights, bell docks, wreck caches, fog marks, mirror charts.
- Visual anchors: gray-green fog, lacquered black hulls, tarnished silver, salt-stained stone, small warm lamps.

### 7. The Ninefold Confluence

The central campaign/skirmish concept region where nine terrain families and six power networks overlap. It is not proof of playability by itself, but it is a strong world location: a shattered mirror basin where roads, rivers, roots, rails, fog lanes, and crystal relays all compete.

Strategic implications:

- This is the right place to test density, not the place to explain every world idea at once.
- Each faction should have at least one credible reason to claim it.
- Object placement should tell the story of overlapping infrastructure: a toll bridge beside a dead relay, a root gate choking a rail spur, a fog dock feeding a ruined mine.

## History Eras

The setting history should be concrete enough to feed artifacts, ruins, campaign motives, and map-object names.

### Era I: The First Measures

Early city-states learned that seasons and tides could be predicted by sky mirrors, huge aerial lenses suspended by old craft and mountain anchors. The mirrors were not divine. They were built infrastructure whose makers are mostly gone.

Design uses:

- Ancient observatories, measuring stones, old anchor pits, broken calibration shrines.
- Artifacts tied to weather, sight, roadfinding, and resource discovery.

### Era II: The Road Oaths

River courts, ferry clans, orchard pilgrims, upland lens keepers, and mine houses built trade compacts around the mirror-regulated world. This age created the legal and ritual languages still used by current factions.

Design uses:

- Tollstones, oath courts, ferry charters, graft laws, mine contracts, harbor bells.
- Campaign conflicts over whose old claim still counts.

### Era III: The Long Shattering

The mirror network cracked slowly. Some mirrors dimmed, some overfocused sunlight, some shed glass into mountains, and some began reflecting memory instead of weather. The world did not end; it became strategically unstable.

Design uses:

- Shard craters, sun scars, memory fog, crystal seams, strange harvests, cursed roads.
- Ruins that are not just treasure boxes, but failed infrastructure.

### Era IV: The Salvage Peace

For a generation, powers survived by repairing local systems. Temporary treaties allowed roads, mills, mines, ferries, groves, and harbors to function. The peace depended on nobody having enough strength to unify the Reach.

Design uses:

- Mixed-region maps with shared ruins and contested neutral sites.
- Heroes who remember cooperation before the war.

### Era V: The Charter War

New mirror fragments have surfaced in the Ninefold Confluence and outer regions. Each faction sees a route to permanent leverage. The player enters when local disputes become a basin-wide war.

Design uses:

- Scenario objectives around capture, denial, escort, restoration, sabotage, and site-linking.
- Artifacts and spells that matter because the world infrastructure is active again.

## Central Conflicts

1. **Restoration versus adaptation**
   - Sunvault and Embercourt lean toward rebuilding order.
   - Mireclaw, Thornwake, and Veilmourn distrust fixed restoration.
   - Brasshollow supports restoration only when it can be contracted and metered.

2. **Fixed roads versus living routes**
   - Embercourt and Brasshollow want roads, rails, and enforceable chokepoints.
   - Mireclaw and Veilmourn profit from routes that shift, hide, or bypass.
   - Thornwake wants routes conditional on root law and land recovery.

3. **Extraction versus renewal**
   - Brasshollow needs deep ore and furnace throughput.
   - Thornwake treats extraction as a debt owed to soil and future growth.
   - Sunvault needs crystals, Embercourt needs wood and grain, Mireclaw needs peat and bodies, Veilmourn needs salvage.

4. **Memory as property**
   - Veilmourn trades in charts, last words, salvage claims, and remembered routes.
   - Embercourt wants public records.
   - Sunvault wants calibrated truth.
   - Mireclaw and Thornwake preserve oral or living memory.
   - Brasshollow wants signed, enforceable memory in contract form.

5. **Local sovereignty versus basin control**
   - Every faction can justify defensive war.
   - Every faction becomes threatening when its local logic scales to the whole map.

## Magical And Metaphysical Rules

Magic in Aurelion Reach is called **accordance**: the practice of making matter, place, memory, and intention agree strongly enough that reality follows.

Core rules:

- Magic needs an anchor. Anchors can be lenses, bells, writs, drums, roots, furnaces, mirrors, bones, road stones, or battlefield formations.
- Magic is easier where history repeats. Old roads, shrines, ruins, battlefields, courts, and mines are naturally powerful because many decisions happened there.
- Magic does not create unlimited matter. It redirects heat, growth, light, fear, memory, morale, motion, and stored potential.
- Overuse leaves residue. Fire leaves ash writs, lens magic leaves glare scars, mire rites leave rot blooms, root law leaves graft knots, furnace work leaves slag spirits, veil rites leave missing memories.
- Spells should have tactical and adventure-map identities. A beacon spell can mark lanes in battle and improve scouting on roads. A root spell can bind stacks in battle and make an overworld route costly. A fog spell can blind archers and conceal a hero path.

### Accord Families

These are not final spell schools yet, but they should guide future magic-system work.

- **Beacon Accord**: signal fire, oath marks, route reveal, morale steadiness, retaliation timing.
- **Mire Accord**: rot, blind, drag, wounded-prey marks, den growth, recovery denial.
- **Lens Accord**: light, resonance, shields, spell cycling, long sight, target calibration.
- **Root Accord**: growth, bind, regeneration, road taxation, site renewal, terrain denial.
- **Furnace Accord**: heat, pressure, armor, repair, explosive release, extraction acceleration.
- **Veil Accord**: fog, displacement, memory theft, salvage sense, morale drain, route bypass.
- **Old Measure Accord**: rare neutral/ancient effects tied to mirror fragments, weather, timekeeping, and world-scale artifacts.

## Resource And Economy Implications

The world supports a richer economy than gold, ore, and wood. Future economy design should treat resources as political and geographic, not just colored costs.

Baseline resources:

- **Gold**: civic liquidity, wages, contracts, bribes, market smoothing.
- **Wood**: roads, town growth, siege frames, boats, root rites.
- **Ore**: armor, machines, fortifications, lens mounts, heavy dwellings.

Foundation resources to evaluate in the economy overhaul:

- **Aetherglass**: mirror and lens fragments used for spells, relays, artifacts, and high-tier magical buildings.
- **Embergrain**: preserved food and lamp fuel used for readiness, recovery, town growth, and long-route logistics.
- **Peatwax**: marsh fuel, sealant, rot rite medium, ferry maintenance, low-tier growth pressure.
- **Verdant Grafts**: living cuttings, root bonds, nursery stock, regeneration structures, terrain-control sites.
- **Brass Scrip**: industrial credit, machine contracts, repair windows, advanced building acceleration.
- **Memory Salt**: salvage ledger medium, fog charts, morale magic, rare artifact activation.

Economy implications by faction:

- Embercourt should prefer gold, wood, embergrain, and road-linked income.
- Mireclaw should prefer peatwax, growth sites, raid spoils, and cheap replacement loops.
- Sunvault should prefer ore, aetherglass, relay-linked spell infrastructure, and quality upgrades.
- Thornwake should prefer wood, verdant grafts, renewal sites, and long-term compounding growth.
- Brasshollow should prefer ore, brass scrip, furnace throughput, and expensive capital projects.
- Veilmourn should prefer memory salt, salvage, scouting rewards, and uneven income spikes.

Map-control implications:

- Mines should not all be one shape. Ore quarry, crystal orchard, peat cut, salvage foundry, graft nursery, and salt wreck-field can share control logic while presenting different route, guard, and faction value.
- Some resources should encourage return routes and defense, not only pickup collection.
- Faction-biased costs should make map position matter. A Sunvault player should care about crystal and ore fronts in a way a Mireclaw player does not.
- Trade and market systems should exist eventually, but faction economies should not flatten into perfect exchange rates.

## Visual Identity Guide

The world should be readable at strategy-map scale first. Concept art can add richness, but the game cannot depend on tiny ornate detail to communicate function.

### Global Shape Language

- Ancient mirror infrastructure: arcs, suspended rings, cracked glass, measuring lines, shadow marks.
- Civic river infrastructure: horizontal mass, locks, towers, rectangular courtyards, warm signal points.
- Marsh infrastructure: low profiles, chain curves, reed clusters, suspended hides, broken causeways.
- Solar infrastructure: angular towers, prismatic facets, stepped terraces, bright focal points.
- Living orchard infrastructure: branching silhouettes, graft bands, root arches, woven toll markers.
- Industrial infrastructure: pipes, rails, gantries, plates, furnace stacks, diagonal braces.
- Fog maritime infrastructure: masts, bells, hull curves, lantern dots, mirror plates, trailing cloth.

### Palette Direction

- Keep terrains distinct without turning the whole game into a one-hue palette.
- Grasslands: fresh greens, warm soil, cream stone, red-orange civic fire accents.
- Mire: green-black water, yellow reed, cold mud, muted bone, occasional poisonous glint.
- Uplands: pale stone, sun gold, blue-violet crystal, hard white highlights.
- Walking Green: deep green, pale bark, thorn red, fruit amber, pilgrimage cloth.
- Brass Deeps: brass, charcoal, iron blue, heated orange, chalk quarry dust.
- Veil Coast: gray-green fog, black lacquer, tarnished silver, salt white, lamp amber.

### Readability Rules

- Major visitable objects need a strong silhouette plus a clear approach side.
- One-shot pickups need small, high-contrast material cues but should not look like UI tokens.
- Towns should be world objects first, not menu badges on terrain.
- Roads, ferries, rails, and fog lanes are strategic information. They must remain legible under objects and scenery.
- Decorative density should frame routes and imply region history without hiding path choices.

## Map Object And World Density Implications

The overworld should feel built and contested. Empty tiles are useful for pacing, but emptiness should be intentional.

### Object Density Targets

For a hand-authored medium zone, aim for a mix like this as a design target, not a validator rule:

- Frequent small pacing objects: caches, carts, cairns, drift salvage, minor shrines.
- Regular durable control objects: mines, yards, nurseries, ferries, relays, watch posts.
- Occasional major decision sites: guarded vaults, banks, old mirror ruins, faction outposts, transit hubs.
- Continuous regional texture: blockers, ruins, roads, water edges, fences, stakes, root walls, rail remnants.

### Object Families Needed By The World

- **Mirror ruins**: ancient neutral sites that can grant magic, artifacts, scouting, or scenario objectives.
- **Route law sites**: tollhouses, ferry chains, root gates, rail switches, fog marks, bridges.
- **Resource fronts**: not just mines, but visible extraction ecosystems with guards and supporting props.
- **Settlement traces**: burned mills, abandoned camps, old contracts, drowned courtyards, orchard graves.
- **Accord shrines**: faction-colored magic sites that teach or empower spell families.
- **Neutral hazards**: unstable weather scars, memory fog pockets, sink roads, glare fields, slag vents.
- **Faction landmarks**: map objects that imply nearby faction influence even outside towns.

### Placement Principles

- Put rewards where route choice matters.
- Use blockers to create readable decisions, not random clutter.
- Let object clusters tell local history: a broken lens above a flooded mine should imply why the area matters.
- Use terrain-specific variants for the same gameplay role so object density builds world identity.
- Avoid text-panel exposition on the map. Let object names, silhouettes, guards, rewards, and routes carry lore.

## Six Faction Hooks

These hooks connect the world foundation to the existing six-faction target without claiming current implementation depth.

### Embercourt League

World role:
- River civic power trying to make law physical through roads, lock gates, beacon towers, granaries, and oath courts.

Conflict pressure:
- Stabilizes trade and protects settlements, but turns every route into a tollable jurisdiction.

Economy hooks:
- Gold, wood, embergrain, road support, fortified crossings, readiness discounts.

Magic hooks:
- Beacon Accord, ash writs, public records, revealed routes, oath retaliation, civic morale.

Object hooks:
- Tollstone weirs, beacon courts, granary locks, bridge bastions, barge slips, signal cairns.

### Mireclaw Covenant

World role:
- Marsh sovereignty network that treats the lowlands as living hunting ground and rejects courthouse maps.

Conflict pressure:
- Protects local freedom and wetland routes, but normalizes raiding, hostage tolls, and wounded-prey law.

Economy hooks:
- Peatwax, raid spoils, den growth, ferry control, cheap replacement, uneven pressure income.

Magic hooks:
- Mire Accord, rot chants, mudglass blindness, drum timing, wounded-prey marks, recovery denial.

Object hooks:
- Chain ferries, reed shrines, peat cuts, beast dens, drum circles, drowned tollhouses.

### Sunvault Compact

World role:
- Disciplined upland society seeking to rebuild the sky-mirror logic through lens relays and harmonic command.

Conflict pressure:
- Can restore weather and sight networks, but would subordinate local life to calibration and hierarchy.

Economy hooks:
- Ore, aetherglass, expensive support buildings, relay nodes, spell access, high-quality upgrades.

Magic hooks:
- Lens Accord, resonance, glare shields, target calibration, long sight, spell cycling.

Object hooks:
- Prism roads, lens galleries, crystal orchards, relay crowns, mirrored terraces, buried observatories.

### Thornwake Concord

World role:
- Migratory orchard law and pilgrim defense network arguing that land must be a political actor.

Conflict pressure:
- Restores damaged places and punishes reckless extraction, but can choke roads, towns, and farms under root veto.

Economy hooks:
- Wood, verdant grafts, renewal sites, long campaigns, recovery, compounding growth.

Magic hooks:
- Root Accord, regeneration, bind, road taxation, site renewal, terrain denial.

Object hooks:
- Root gates, graft halls, thorn tolls, living orchards, nursery shrines, pilgrim clearings.

### Brasshollow Combine

World role:
- Furnace-contract society that turns ore, pressure, debt, and engineering into territorial certainty.

Conflict pressure:
- Builds durable infrastructure and reliable machines, but treats land, labor, and war as payable obligations.

Economy hooks:
- Ore, brass scrip, extraction chains, expensive capital projects, machine repair, siege staging.

Magic hooks:
- Furnace Accord, heat pressure, armor, repair, explosive release, extraction acceleration.

Object hooks:
- Contract foundries, pressure rails, pump houses, slag roads, furnace chapels, quarry courts.

### Veilmourn Armada

World role:
- Fog-bound fleet culture claiming salvage rights, hidden routes, and memory trade across coast and inland mist.

Conflict pressure:
- Preserves routes others cannot hold and rescues value from disaster, but erodes fixed borders and public truth.

Economy hooks:
- Memory salt, salvage, scouting rewards, fog routes, ambush value, uneven power spikes.

Magic hooks:
- Veil Accord, displacement, memory theft, morale drain, route bypass, salvage sense.

Object hooks:
- Bell docks, mirror shoals, obituary vaults, wreck fields, black-sail slips, fog chart houses.

## Design Requirements For Next Slices

Faction identity work should now revise or extend `docs/factions-content-bible.md` against this world foundation. It should preserve useful existing names only when they still fit the world role and should document any intended rename before JSON migration.

Economy work should test whether the proposed foundation resources are too many, too few, or need grouping before content implementation.

Overworld object work should convert the object-family implications above into concrete taxonomy, footprints, visit rules, guard expectations, and biome/faction variants.

Magic work should turn accord families into spell schools or categories with tactical and adventure-map roles.

Artifact work should favor world infrastructure artifacts: mirror fragments, old measures, toll writs, ferry bells, graft knives, furnace seals, fog charts, and route stones.

Art-direction and image-generation work should start with broad world mood and region studies before individual units, so faction art does not drift into six disconnected aesthetics.
